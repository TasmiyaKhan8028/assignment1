Quaid e Azam1

Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah, the founding father of Pakistan, was a remarkable leader and statesman who played a pivotal role in the creation of the independent state of Pakistan in 1947. Born on December 25, 1876, in Karachi, Jinnah belonged to a well-educated and affluent family. Trained as a lawyer, he quickly rose to prominence in the legal and political spheres. Jinnah's vision for a separate nation for Muslims in British India became the driving force behind the Pakistan Movement. He was a staunch advocate for the rights of Muslims and worked tirelessly to safeguard their political, social, and economic interests. His unwavering commitment to the principles of justice, equality, and religious freedom earned him the title of "Quaid-e-Azam," meaning "Great Leader." As the leader of the All-India Muslim League, Jinnah skillfully negotiated with the British and other political leaders to secure the creation of Pakistan. His leadership during the challenging and tumultuous times leading up to the partition of India showcased his determination and political acumen. On August 14, 1947, Pakistan emerged as an independent nation, and Jinnah became its first Governor-General until his death on September 11, 1948.


Allama Iqbal2

Allama Iqbal, full name Muhammad Iqbal, was a renowned philosopher, poet, and politician in British India who is widely regarded as having inspired the Pakistan Movement. He was born on November 9, 1877, in Sialkot, which is now part of Pakistan, and he passed away on April 21, 1938. Iqbal is often referred to as Mufakkir-e-Pakistan (The Thinker of Pakistan) and Shair-e-Mashriq (The Poet of the East). His poetry, written primarily in Persian and Urdu, reflects his deep philosophical thoughts, spiritual insight, and a call for the revival of Islamic values. Iqbal's work had a significant impact on the intellectual and political landscape of the Indian subcontinent. He played a key role in shaping the ideological foundation of Pakistan, advocating for a separate nation for Muslims. His famous poem "Lab Pe Aati Hai Dua Ban Ke Tamanna Meri" is widely recited in schools across Pakistan
